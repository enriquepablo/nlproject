Documentation for nl¶

nl is a python library for logic programming. As normal with logic programming systems, it has a declarative API. It follows a productive paradigm, that stems from its internal use of the CLIPS expert system, and its basic usage can be summarized in four steps:

  • Define terms to be used in sentences and rules;
  • Build sentences and rules, and enter them into its kowledge base;
  • Extend the knowledge base to all its logical consecuences;
  • Query the knowledge base.

In nl we can build 2 classes of sentences: copulative, and non-copulative sentences.

Copulative sentences have two parts: a proper name, and a common noun. They are semantically equivalent to English copulative sentences, such as “john is a man”, or “yellow is a colour”.

Non-copulative sentences (which I will hereforth call just sentences for short) are made up of 3 parts: a subject, a predicate, and a time expression. The subject would be a proper name, already declared in a copulative sentence. The predicate would be made up of a verb, and a number of named modifiers. They would be equivalent to non conjugated English predicates, of the kind that Tarzan of the apes accustomed to utter. For example, “go from London to Paris”, where “go” would be the verb, “London” would be a modifier and “from” its name, and “Paris” would be another modifier named “to”. Finally, time expressions would correspond to the conjugation of verbs, and can be of two kinds: instants, and intervals. A complete example of a “sort of English” sentence that could be expressed in nl might be “Tarzan go from London to Paris between <date1> and <date2>”.
